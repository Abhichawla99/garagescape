The McKenzie Towne Lane Garage Challenge

McKenzie Towne was developed from the late 1990s through the 2000s as a new urbanism experiment — a reaction to suburban sprawl. The design prioritizes walkability and community feel: homes face the street with front porches, and vehicles access the garage from a rear lane. The tradeoff is that these laneway garages are typically 16x20 or 18x20 ft — significantly smaller than the attached double garages you'd find in communities like Panorama Hills or Aspen Woods.

Here's the problem: the house itself often lacks a basement, or has a reduced-footprint basement that doesn't hold much. Everything that would normally live in a basement — seasonal decorations, sports equipment, camping gear, tools, overflow from the kitchen and living areas — ends up in the garage. A 16x20 garage trying to store a vehicle plus everything else in the house is a recipe for chaos.

The families who live here are often young professionals with growing kids, active lifestyles, and not a lot of time to deal with the garage. Summer bikes, winter hockey gear, camping equipment, tools for maintaining the home, and a year's worth of seasonal items all need homes. The lane garage is where it all lands.

Our approach is to design a full-height, zone-based storage system that treats the lane garage as what it actually is: the household's primary storage infrastructure. Why are McKenzie Towne garages so difficult to organize?

McKenzie Towne was built as a new urbanism community with rear-lane garages — typically 16x20 or 18x20 ft — that are detached and accessed from the back alley. Many homes also lack a full basement, so the garage becomes the only meaningful storage space for the household. That's a lot of pressure on a small footprint, and without a professional system it quickly becomes unusable.

Can you fit enough storage in a small 16x20 lane garage?

Absolutely. Small garages benefit most from professional organization because every decision matters. We use the full wall height, install overhead ceiling storage for seasonal items, and zone the space so daily-use items are always reachable. Seasonal rotation is the cornerstone of our McKenzie Towne projects. We design clearly labelled ceiling-level zones for off-season items and eye-level zones for the current season's active gear.
